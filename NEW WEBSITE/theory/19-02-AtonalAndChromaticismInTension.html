Section 19.2 Atonal and Chromaticism in Tension

The deliberate abandonment of traditional tonal centers and the systematic exploration of chromatic harmony represent some of music's most powerful tools for creating psychological tension and dramatic effect. From the late Romantic period's chromatic saturation to the complete atonality of the Second Viennese School, composers have discovered that dissonance and harmonic ambiguity can express complex emotional states impossible to achieve through conventional tonal means. In visual media, these techniques have become essential for conveying psychological depth, creating suspense, and representing the darker aspects of human experience. Understanding atonal and chromatic techniques is crucial for analyzing music across multiple Areas of Study, particularly in contexts requiring sophisticated emotional expression and dramatic intensity. This chapter examines the theoretical foundations, compositional techniques, and practical applications of atonality and chromaticism in creating musical tension.

Subsection 19.2.1 Chromatic Harmony and Tonal Dissolution

Advanced Chromatic Techniques

Chromatic Saturation: The systematic use of all twelve chromatic pitches within a relatively short time span, creating harmonic density and tonal ambiguity.

Key Chromatic Devices:

  • Chromatic Voice Leading: Stepwise motion through semitones creating smooth but dissonant progressions

  • Chromatic Mediant Relationships: Root movements by major or minor thirds, weakening tonal gravity

  • Augmented Sixth Chords: Multiple forms creating harmonic ambiguity and enharmonic possibilities

  • Neapolitan Relationships: Flattened supertonic harmony and its extensions

  • Tritone Substitution: Replacing dominant function with chords a tritone away

Chromatic Scales and Modes:

  • Chromatic Scale: All twelve semitones in ascending or descending order

  • Octatonic Scale: Alternating whole and half steps (8 pitches)

  • Hexatonic Scale: Alternating minor thirds and semitones (6 pitches)

  • Whole-Tone Scale: Exclusively whole-step intervals (6 pitches)

Harmonic Techniques for Tension:

  • Unresolved Dissonance: Avoiding traditional resolution patterns

  • False Relations: Simultaneous or successive chromatic contradictions

  • Chromatic Clusters: Dense semitone groupings creating harsh sonorities

  • Enharmonic Modulation: Sudden key changes through enharmonic reinterpretation

The Process of Tonal Dissolution

Historical Development: The gradual breakdown of traditional tonality from Wagner through Schoenberg, representing a systematic exploration of harmonic possibilities.

Stages of Tonal Dissolution:

  • Extended Tonality (Late Romantic): Chromatic harmony within tonal framework

  • Suspended Tonality (Early 20th Century): Ambiguous tonal centers with periodic clarification

  • Atonal Chromaticism (1908-1920): Complete abandonment of tonal hierarchy

  • Serial Organization (1920s onward): Systematic ordering of chromatic materials

Analytical Approaches:

  • Pitch-Class Set Theory: Mathematical analysis of atonal relationships

  • Voice-Leading Analysis: Tracking chromatic motion between voices

  • Harmonic Rhythm Analysis: Examining the pace of harmonic change

  • Tension and Release Patterns: Identifying non-tonal structural principles

Psychological Effects:

  • Anxiety and Unease: Lack of tonal stability creating psychological discomfort

  • Suspense: Harmonic ambiguity maintaining dramatic tension

  • Disorientation: Absence of familiar harmonic landmarks

  • Intensity: Chromatic density increasing emotional pressure

Subsection 19.2.2 Bitonality and Polytonality

Bitonality: Simultaneous Key Centers

Definition: Bitonality is the simultaneous use of two different key centers, creating harmonic conflict and tension through the juxtaposition of competing tonal systems.

Types of Bitonal Relationships:

  • Tritone Bitonality: Keys separated by a tritone (maximum tonal conflict)

  • Semitone Bitonality: Adjacent keys creating chromatic friction

  • Third-Related Bitonality: Keys related by major or minor thirds

  • Perfect Fifth Bitonality: Keys in dominant relationship but treated independently

Compositional Techniques:

  • Layered Bitonality: Different instruments or voices in different keys

  • Alternating Bitonality: Rapid switching between two tonal centers

  • Merged Bitonality: Gradual blending of two key areas

  • Structural Bitonality: Large-scale formal sections in different keys

Notable Examples:

  • Stravinsky's "Petrushka" (1911): C major and F# major simultaneously

  • Milhaud's "Saudades do Brasil" (1920-21): Systematic bitonal exploration

  • Bartók's "Mikrokosmos" (1926-39): Pedagogical bitonal exercises

  • Ives's "The Unanswered Question" (1908): Tonal and atonal layers

Polytonality: Multiple Key Centers

Definition: Polytonality extends bitonality to include three or more simultaneous key centers, creating complex harmonic textures and maximum tonal ambiguity.

Organizational Strategies:

  • Instrumental Separation: Different instruments or sections in different keys

  • Register Separation: Different key centers in different octaves

  • Rhythmic Separation: Different keys associated with different rhythmic patterns

  • Textural Separation: Melody, harmony, and bass in different keys

Analytical Challenges:

  • Perceptual Hierarchy: Determining which tonal centers are most prominent

  • Harmonic Resultants: Understanding the combined effect of multiple keys

  • Structural Function: Analyzing formal relationships in polytonal contexts

  • Performance Considerations: Practical challenges for musicians

Dramatic Applications:

  • Chaos and Confusion: Multiple keys representing disorder or conflict

  • Layered Reality: Different tonal planes representing different perspectives

  • Psychological Complexity: Multiple keys reflecting complex mental states

  • Supernatural Elements: Polytonality suggesting otherworldly phenomena

Subsection 19.2.3 Whole-Tone Scales and Harmonic Applications

Whole-Tone Scale Properties

Structure: The whole-tone scale consists of six pitches separated by whole steps, creating a symmetrical structure with unique harmonic properties.

Two Whole-Tone Collections:

  • Collection I: C-D-E-F#-G#-A# (and enharmonic equivalents)

  • Collection II: C#-D#-F-G-A-B (and enharmonic equivalents)

Harmonic Characteristics:

  • No Perfect Fifths: Absence of traditional consonant intervals

  • Augmented Triads: Primary harmonic sonority within the scale

  • Dominant Seventh Chords: With raised fifth, creating augmented dominant harmony

  • French Sixth Chords: Natural harmonic formation within whole-tone context

Melodic Properties:

  • Floating Quality: Lack of leading tones creating ambiguous direction

  • Dreamy Character: Impressionistic and ethereal melodic quality

  • Symmetrical Patterns: Equal intervallic relationships throughout

  • Limited Transposition: Only two distinct whole-tone collections possible

Impressionist and Symbolic Applications

Debussy's Innovations: Claude Debussy (1862-1918) pioneered the systematic use of whole-tone scales in Western art music, creating new possibilities for harmonic color and atmospheric effect.

Characteristic Uses:

  • Water and Fluidity: Representing flowing, liquid motion

  • Dreams and Fantasy: Creating otherworldly, surreal atmospheres

  • Ambiguity and Mystery: Suggesting uncertainty and the unknown

  • Exotic Locations: Evoking distant or imaginary places

Notable Examples:

  • Debussy's "Voiles" (1909): Extensive whole-tone exploration

  • Debussy's "La Mer" (1903-05): Oceanic imagery through whole-tone harmony

  • Ravel's "Daphnis et Chloé" (1909-12): Pastoral and mythological associations

  • Liszt's "Bagatelle sans tonalité" (1885): Early whole-tone experimentation

Contemporary Applications:

  • Film Scoring: Dream sequences, supernatural elements, psychological states

  • Jazz Harmony: Advanced chord substitutions and improvisational frameworks

  • Video Game Music: Fantasy environments and magical atmospheres

  • Ambient Music: Creating floating, meditative soundscapes

Subsection 19.2.4 Atonal Composition and Serial Techniques

Free Atonality (1908-1920)

Definition: Free atonality refers to music that avoids traditional tonal centers without employing systematic organizational principles, relying instead on intuitive compositional decisions.

Schoenberg's Breakthrough: Arnold Schoenberg's abandonment of tonality in works like "Three Piano Pieces, Op. 11" (1909) marked a revolutionary moment in Western music history.

Compositional Principles:

  • Avoidance of Tonal References: Systematic elimination of traditional harmonic progressions

  • Chromatic Saturation: Use of all twelve pitch classes with equal frequency

  • Motivic Development: Reliance on intervallic and rhythmic patterns for coherence

  • Textural Variation: Dynamic and registral contrasts providing structural articulation

Analytical Approaches:

  • Pitch-Class Set Analysis: Identifying recurring interval patterns

  • Motivic Analysis: Tracking thematic development and transformation

  • Textural Analysis: Examining density, register, and dynamic relationships

  • Formal Analysis: Understanding non-tonal structural principles

Twelve-Tone Serial Technique

Development: Schoenberg developed the twelve-tone technique in the early 1920s as a systematic method for organizing atonal music, ensuring equal treatment of all chromatic pitches.

Basic Principles:

  • Tone Row (Series): Ordered arrangement of all twelve chromatic pitches

  • No Repetition: Each pitch class used once before any repetition

  • Four Row Forms: Prime (P), Inversion (I), Retrograde (R), Retrograde-Inversion (RI)

  • Transposition: Each row form available at all twelve pitch levels

Compositional Applications:

  • Melodic Lines: Row forms creating thematic material

  • Harmonic Structures: Vertical combinations of row segments

  • Formal Organization: Row relationships providing structural coherence

  • Textural Layering: Multiple row forms in different voices

Expressive Possibilities:

  • Psychological Intensity: Atonal harmony expressing extreme emotional states

  • Intellectual Rigor: Mathematical precision in compositional organization

  • Modernist Aesthetics: Rejection of romantic sentimentality

  • Universal Language: Systematic approach transcending cultural boundaries

Subsection 19.2.5 Dramatic Applications and Psychological Effects

Atonal Techniques in Film and Television

Horror and Suspense: Atonal and chromatic techniques have become standard tools for creating fear, anxiety, and psychological tension in visual media.

Specific Applications:

  • Jump Scares: Sudden atonal clusters creating shock effects

  • Psychological Thriller: Chromatic harmony reflecting mental instability

  • Supernatural Horror: Whole-tone scales suggesting otherworldly presence

  • Dystopian Futures: Serial techniques representing dehumanized societies

Notable Film Composers:

  • Bernard Herrmann: "Psycho" (1960), innovative use of string clusters

  • György Ligeti: "2001: A Space Odyssey" (1968), avant-garde techniques

  • Krzysztof Penderecki: "The Shining" (1980), extended techniques and clusters

  • Jonny Greenwood: "There Will Be Blood" (2007), contemporary atonal approaches

Psychological Mechanisms:

  • Cognitive Dissonance: Harmonic instability creating mental discomfort

  • Expectation Violation: Avoiding resolution patterns disrupting listener predictions

  • Physiological Response: Dissonance triggering stress responses

  • Cultural Conditioning: Learned associations between atonality and negative emotions

Contemporary and Cross-Genre Applications

Art Music: Continued exploration of atonal and chromatic techniques in contemporary classical composition.

Popular Music:

  • Progressive Rock: Bands like King Crimson incorporating atonal elements

  • Metal Genres: Death metal and black metal using chromatic harmony for intensity

  • Experimental Electronic: Digital manipulation creating new forms of atonality

  • Ambient Music: Chromatic clusters in atmospheric compositions

Video Game Music:

  • Horror Games: Atonal techniques for fear and suspense

  • Sci-Fi Settings: Chromatic harmony suggesting alien environments

  • Psychological Thrillers: Whole-tone scales for mental instability

  • Boss Battles: Intense chromatic writing for climactic encounters

Analytical Exercises:

  • Chromatic Analysis: Identifying and categorizing chromatic techniques

  • Tension Mapping: Charting harmonic tension and release patterns

  • Comparative Study: Examining different composers' approaches to atonality

  • Creative Application: Composing original atonal passages for specific dramatic effects

Subsection 19.2.6 Conclusion and Further Study

Atonal and chromatic techniques represent a fundamental expansion of music's expressive vocabulary, providing composers with powerful tools for conveying complex psychological states and dramatic situations. From the systematic chromaticism of late Romanticism to the complete atonality of the Second Viennese School, these approaches have revolutionized musical expression and continue to influence contemporary composition across all genres. Understanding these techniques is essential for analyzing music that seeks to express the darker, more complex aspects of human experience, particularly in contexts where traditional tonal harmony proves inadequate. As visual media continues to explore psychological depth and emotional complexity, atonal and chromatic techniques remain indispensable tools for musical storytelling and dramatic expression.

Recommended Further Study:

  • Detailed analysis of Schoenberg's atonal period (1908-1920)

  • Comparative study of twelve-tone techniques across different composers

  • Exploration of extended chromatic harmony in film scoring

  • Investigation of whole-tone applications in impressionist music

  • Practical composition exercises using atonal and chromatic techniques

Mastery of this section is essential, as the Baroque era forms the foundation for Western musical analysis and introduces numerous fundamental theoretical concepts required throughout the course.

4. Language, Literacy, and Theory: Area of Study 1 (Baroque Music)

4.1. Specific Terminology: Genre-specific vocabulary (e.g., tala, swing, maqam, ostinato).

You must acquire and use terminology specific to Baroque structure, ensemble, and performance practices to accurately describe music from this era.

Terminology FocusDefinition/ContextSource Citations
Ensemble & RolesThe ensemble is divided into the ripieno (the full orchestral group, also called tutti) and the concertino (the small group of soloists in a concerto grosso). The ripieno performs the main ritornello theme.
StructureRitornello form is a common structure, especially in concertos, where the orchestral ritornello theme alternates with solo sections called episodes. You must also know structures like fugue and French overture.
Texture & DevicesPolyphonic or contrapuntal texture is highly characteristic, often involving imitation. Other essential devices include suspensions, sequence, and the rhythmic device hemiola.
DynamicsYou must know terraced dynamics, which refers to sudden changes from loud (forte) to quiet (piano) or vice versa, stemming from performance conventions of the time.
OrnamentsOrnamentation is central to Baroque melody. Specific ornaments you must be able to recognize and name include the trill and mordent.

4.2. Lyrics & Text Analysis: The study of the words, including their poetic structure, narrative, imagery, and symbolism.

When analyzing Baroque vocal forms (like opera or oratorio) or programmatic instrumental music, you must understand how musical features are used to convey meaning.

  • Vocal Setting: You must analyze the composer's choice of word-setting. This includes syllabic settings (one note per syllable) or melisma (the setting of one syllable to many notes in the melody line).
  • Word-Painting: The technique of word-painting‚Äîwhere music illustrates the meaning of the words‚Äîis often employed, particularly in arias and oratorios.
  • Programme Music: You will study programmatic instrumental music where the music is used to depict external ideas or images. For instance, Vivaldi uses high register, polyphonic texture, and ornamentation (trills/mordents) to suggest the "singing of the birds" in Spring.
  • Vocal Genres: The overall historical context includes opera (a secular drama set to music, acted and sung), oratorio (a sacred setting), and cantata.

4.3. Notation System: How music is recorded‚Äîstaff notation, cipher, neumes, graphic scores, etc.

In the Baroque period, Western music was primarily recorded using the precursor to modern staff notation.

  • Staff Notation: You are expected to read and understand the fundamentals of staff notation, including the treble, bass, and alto clefs. Score-reading is a core skill developed through Focus Works.
  • Figured Bass: A unique Baroque notation system called figured bass was developed as a shorthand for writing chords. This notation consists of a bass line written on the staff, accompanied by numbers (figures) placed below the notes. These figures indicate the chords that the continuo player (harpsichord, organ, or lute) should improvise, providing the harmonic basis for the piece.

4.4. Oral/Aural Tradition: Transmission of music through listening and imitation rather than written form.

While music was notated, Baroque performance heavily relied on customary practice, which is an aspect of aural tradition.

  • Performer Interpretation: The basso continuo part was fundamentally based on performer interpretation, as the player was expected to use their skill and musicianship to improvise chords and decorate the musical texture guided only by the figured bass.
  • Dynamic Convention: Dynamic markings were often omitted in scores because musicians knew the expected conventions. This necessary knowledge was passed on implicitly or through training, which is a form of aural transmission.
  • Ornamentation: Performers were expected to add ornamentation and extra notes to the written score.

4.5. Music Theory Framework: The theoretical system underpinning the music (e.g., Western harmony, Javanese slendro/pelog).

The Baroque period established the foundational theoretical framework for Western classical music.

  • Key System: The defining change was the widespread adoption of the major-minor key system.
  • Harmony and Tonality: Harmony is primarily diatonic and functional, meaning chords have a clear role (e.g., leading to a cadence). You must recognize modulation to related keys, such as the dominant, subdominant, or relative major/minor.
  • Mandatory Concepts: Key devices include pedal notes (tonic pedal, dominant pedal) and suspensions. You must identify standard cadences: perfect (V-I), imperfect (II/IV-V), and interrupted (V-VI).
  • Texture: The Baroque focus requires a deep understanding of polyphonic (or contrapuntal) writing, involving multiple independent melodic lines, in contrast with homophonic (melody and accompaniment) texture.

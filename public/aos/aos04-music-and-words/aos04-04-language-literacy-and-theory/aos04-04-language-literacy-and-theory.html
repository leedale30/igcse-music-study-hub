Aos04 04 Language Literacy And Theory

This comprehensive framework, structured according to Section 4 (Language, Literacy, and Theory) of the analysis lexicon, ensures candidates are fully prepared to demonstrate sophisticated analytical and descriptive skills (AO1) regarding vocal music.

This area mandates the study of Art song for solo voice, music for choirs, popular song, and songs from musicals.

4. Language, Literacy, and Theory: Area of Study 4 (Music and Words)

4.1. Specific Terminology: Genre-specific vocabulary

You must acquire and consistently use terminology specific to modern song structure, vocal setting, and music production technology.

Terminology FocusDefinition and ApplicationSource Citations
Word-Setting TechniquesMelisma (setting one syllable of text to many notes in the melody line) and Syllabic (setting one note per syllable). Also includes Rap (rhythmic, spoken delivery of lyrics).
Popular Song StructureSpecific section names are mandatory: Intro, Verse, Chorus, Bridge / middle eight, and Outro. Structures include Strophic (same music repeats for each verse) and Through-composed (music continuously changes, following the narrative).
Melodic DevicesHook (a catchy, memorable sequence of notes) and Riff (a short, repeated pattern, often instrumental) are crucial elements of popular song. You must also recognize Call and response (a musical dialogue where one part answers another).
Choral TexturesAntiphonal texture (where one group responds to another) and the division of voices into a Double choir are common in choral music.
Music TechnologySample, loop, chorus, reverb, and delay are electronic effects and devices used to enhance and produce sound. Production techniques include overdubbing, double-tracking, and multi-tracking.
TempoTempo can be measured using BPM (beats per minute), particularly in popular and electronic genres. You must also recognize tempo changes like accelerando and rallentando.

4.2. Lyrics & Text Analysis: The study of the words, including their poetic structure, narrative, imagery, and symbolism.

This is the central analytical pillar of AOS 4. Candidates must explore the ways composers set words to music, focusing on musical features that enhance the meaning of the text.

  • Word-Painting: This is a crucial technique where specific musical features (like pitch, dynamics, or melody contour) directly illustrate the literal meaning of individual words or phrases. For example, using ascending scales in the piano to suggest the word 'reach'.
  • Melisma and Syllabic Setting: The choice between melisma (many notes per syllable, often for expressive elongation) and syllabic setting (one note per syllable, often for clarity or speed) is a conscious decision by the composer to affect the text's delivery.
  • Emotional Conveyance: Composers use harmony (e.g., chromaticism or dissonance) and tonality (minor key for melancholic feelings) to convey the emotional content or mood of the lyrics.

4.3. Notation System: How music is recorded‚Äîstaff notation, cipher, neumes, graphic scores, etc.

While Western music is traditionally documented using staff notation, modern genres rely heavily on recording techniques and non-traditional methods.

  • Staff Notation: Standard Western notation is used for Art Song, Choral music, and scores from Musicals. Candidates must be able to read notation that includes all rhythmic note values (including triplets and dotted rhythms), common time signatures (duple, triple, quadruple, simple, and compound).
  • Commentary Alternative: For composition (Component 3), a detailed written commentary describing the composition's structure and content can be submitted as an alternative to a written score, which is particularly relevant when composing in genres like popular song or EDM where traditional notation may be less appropriate.

4.4. Oral/Aural Tradition: Transmission of music through listening and imitation rather than written form.

Aural skills are paramount in this area, both for analysis and transmission in contemporary and popular contexts.

  • Aural Analysis (AO1): The examination requires accurate aural recognition of key elements, such as structure (identifying where the chorus returns or a bridge occurs) and texture (recognizing homophonic texture or antiphonal choral singing).
  • Pop/Digital Transmission: Popular songs and often pieces from Musicals are widely distributed via broadcast media and digital streaming platforms, where the primary mode of transmission and consumption is aural, not written.
  • Improvisation: Solo vocal parts, particularly in popular song and some Art Song traditions, may incorporate improvisation.

4.5. Music Theory Framework: The theoretical system underpinning the music (e.g., Western harmony, Javanese slendro/pelog).

Music in this area relies on the Western tonal system but expands it with modal and chromatic elements.

  • Tonality and Harmony: You must recognize major and minor keys. While harmony is often diatonic, the use of chromatic harmony and dissonance is common, particularly in contemporary choral music and Art Song, to heighten expression.
  • Chord Vocabulary: Required knowledge includes primary and secondary chords, along with dominant seventh and diminished seventh chords.
  • Cadences: You must aurally identify the common harmonic progressions: perfect, imperfect, and interrupted cadences.
  • Scales: Recognition of modal scales is often necessary in Art Song and contemporary choral music, alongside major, minor, and chromatic scales.
  • Texture: You must distinguish between monophonic, homophonic / melody and accompaniment, polyphonic, and antiphonal textures in vocal ensembles.

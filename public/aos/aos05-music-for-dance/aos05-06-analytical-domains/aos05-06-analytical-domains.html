6. Analytical Perspectives: Area of Study 5: Music for Dance

This area requires candidates to analyze how musical features, technology, specific instruments, and social context contribute to the three distinct dance genres: Tango, Salsa, and Electronic Dance Music (EDM).

6.1. Genre and Style: Classification and stylistic traits‚Äîbaroque, reggae, gamelan, trap, etc.

Classification and Repertoire

AOS 5 focuses on music explicitly written for dance, spanning both traditional and contemporary styles. 1. Tango: An urban genre originating in Buenos Aires (late 1800s), considered the representative music and dance form of Argentina. 2. Salsa: Dance music emerging in East Harlem, New York City, drawing heavily on Afro-Cuban and Latin American elements. 3. EDM: A global genre defined by electronic instruments and repetitive rhythms, originating in clubs in the late 1970s and early 1980s.

Stylistic Traits (Identifying Features)

Candidates must accurately identify characteristics specific to each genre using appropriate musical terminology:

  • Rhythm and Metre:
  • Tango: Typically employs duple metre (2/4 or 4/4) and features defining syncopated rhythms (sincopa). Phrases often begin with an anacrusis (upbeat).
  • Salsa: Characterized by relentless rhythmic drive. The interlocking polyrhythm is structured by the two-bar rhythmic ostinato played by the clav√©, known as the son clav√© (forward/reverse clave). The bass often uses a distinctive anticipated bass pattern, emphasizing off-beats.
  • EDM: Defined by a steady four-to-the-floor rhythm, where the kick drum hits on every beat in 4/4 time. Offbeat rhythms and syncopated percussion (shakers, hi-hats) are added to build texture.
  • Instrumentation and Medium:
  • Tango: The distinctive instrument is the bandoneon (an accordion-like instrument with buttons). Ensembles may be instrumental or vocal, often including strings, piano, and double bass (orquesta tipica).
  • Salsa: Features cuatro (Puerto Rican lute-like instrument) and clav√©s (hardwood sticks). Ensembles are often large (10‚Äì14+ members) incorporating vocals, Afro-Cuban percussion (congas, timbales), piano, bass, and prominent brass instruments (trumpets, trombones).
  • EDM: Primarily relies on Music Technology. Key equipment includes synthesizers (e.g., Moog) to generate sounds, drum machines (e.g., Roland TR808) for rhythmic patterns, and Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs) for composition and mixing.
  • Structure:
  • Tango: Tends to have two or three main sections, with phrases grouped regularly (e.g., 2, 4, or 8 bars). Sections are often repeated with varied scoring.
  • Salsa: Typically starts with an introduction, followed by a verse and a chorus section known as the montuno. The montuno features call-and-response between a lead vocalist and coro (chorus singers) over a repeating harmonic progression.
  • EDM: Structure is dictated by energy, featuring alternating builds (rising volume, increasing texture) and climactic drops (release of tension via powerful basslines and drum fills).

6.2. Aesthetic Values: What is considered beautiful, powerful, or meaningful within the tradition.

The aesthetic value of dance music is measured by its capacity to induce movement, express kinetic energy, and achieve specific sonic qualities.

  • Kinetic Energy and Drive: Meaningfulness is derived from the music's ability to maintain a strong, relentless pulse and rhythmic continuity, essential for dancing.
  • Emotional Intensity: In Tango, aesthetic power is achieved through expressive musical devices like glissando and portamento, conveying passion and melancholia, often supported by minor keys.
  • Sonic Engineering (EDM): Aesthetic value relies heavily on technical execution and sound manipulation. Power is built through the effective use of production effects like filtering (e.g., low-pass filters for builds), reverb (for spaciousness), and delay (for rhythmic interest). The dramatic tension and euphoric release of the builds and drops are central aesthetic devices.

6.3. Audience Reception: How listeners and communities engage with and interpret the music.

Audience engagement is actively physical, defined by the venue and the global method of distribution. Candidates must possess the aural awareness, perception, and discrimination to analyze recorded extracts.

  • Performance Contexts:
  • Tango/Salsa: Initially associated with local, lower-middle-class community spaces (bars, caf√©s, bodegas, streets). Following globalization (via film, radio, and commercial recordings), they shifted to cosmopolitan public theatres, large stadium venues, and concert halls worldwide.
  • EDM: Engaged through DJ sets (mixing pre-recorded tracks) or live production sets (real-time manipulation using electronic instruments/software) in clubs and massive festivals.
  • Interpretation through Movement: The primary interpretation is physical movement and dance, influenced by the DJ's goal to create a continuous flow of music.
  • Global Transmission: All modern dance forms are widely distributed through digital streaming platforms (YouTube, Spotify, Apple Music), allowing for global access and recognition. EDM producers often learn through 'recreating' popular tracks, which acts as an entry point into a community of practice.

6.4. Political/Social Commentary: Messages or movements embedded in the music (e.g., protest songs, feminist punk).

This perspective is particularly relevant for Tango and Salsa, which emerged from distinct social environments.

  • Salsa and Social Identity: Salsa became a symbol of pan-Latino ethnic identity among immigrants in New York. Early salsa lyrics frequently recounted the hard lives and discontent of working-class Spanish-speaking immigrants, using strong rhythmic drive and strident brass to reflect the experiences of violence and discontent. Later, lyrics focused more on romantic themes.
  • Tango and Migration: The development of Tango was intrinsically linked to the social environment of Buenos Aires and the arrival of immigrants and African slaves, whose cultural traditions merged to shape the genre.
  • Wider Relevance: The syllabus encourages using local and popular musical styles and examples, affirming that dance music can reflect contemporary social trends and cultural shifts.

6.5. Geographic Influence: How climate, landscape, and migration shape musical identity.

Geographic and demographic influences are key to understanding the origins of Tango and Salsa.

  • Migration and Hybridization: Tango‚Äôs identity was shaped by the migration of a large number of immigrants from Italy, Spain, Eastern Europe, and African slaves into Buenos Aires, resulting in the merging of diverse music and dance traditions.
  • Urban Origins (Salsa): Salsa‚Äôs identity is rooted in the specific urban geography of East Harlem, NYC, emerging after WWII due to the arrival of Puerto Rican and other Central/Latin American immigrants.
  • Globalized Production (EDM): While geographically diffused, EDM‚Äôs identity is primarily shaped by Music Technology and Digital Audio Workstations (DAWs), rather than traditional landscape influences. It is defined as a global cultural movement.

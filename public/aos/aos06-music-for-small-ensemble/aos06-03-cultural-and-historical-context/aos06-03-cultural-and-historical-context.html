Aos06 03 Cultural And Historical Context

This meticulous breakdown highlights the mandated cultural and historical context necessary for demonstrating superior analytical and aural skills (AO1) in your examination.

This area requires you to master the contexts, instruments, and characteristic musical features of three specific non-Western traditions: Silk and bamboo ensembles (China), Hindustani classical music (India), and Arab takht ensembles (Arab world).

3. Cultural and Historical Context: Area of Study 6 (Music for Small Ensemble)

3.1. Cultural Function: The role music plays in society.

This section explores the crucial roles that music from these specific small ensembles plays within its native society, moving beyond pure entertainment.

3.1.1.1. Ritual

Music is often tied to ritualistic or auspicious occasions in these cultures. For example, in the Hindustani tradition, musicians such as the LangƒÅs and MƒÅnganiyƒÅrs were traditionally hired to perform for important family and religious events, including ritual feasts and marriage ceremonies.

3.1.1.2. Celebration

All three ensemble types serve a function of celebration and social gathering:
  • Hindustani Music: Music performed by LangƒÅs and MƒÅnganiyƒÅrs musicians was traditionally played for mangal (joyous and auspicious occasions), such as birthdays and religious celebrations.
  • Chinese Music: Silk and Bamboo (Sizhu) music was traditionally performed in social settings like tea-houses [67, 18(a)].

3.1.1.5. Social Identity

The music of these ensembles is integral to reflecting and maintaining distinct social and regional identity. Candidates are studying the characteristic sounds and structures that define Chinese, Indian, and Arab musical language.

3.2. Historical Background: The evolution of the style or tradition and its socio-political influences.

This covers how the musical traditions evolved and where they fit socially and historically.

  • Transmission of Music: A key element is the method by which music is passed down. While Western music relies heavily on staff notation, many non-Western traditions rely on oral/aural transmission. For instance, Hindustani classical music utilizes the guru-shishya system (teacher-disciple).
  • Traditional vs. Modern Context (Chinese Sizhu): The Chinese Silk and Bamboo tradition has undergone significant evolution. It was originally characterized by being played by amateur musicians in tea-houses [67, 18(a)]. However, it has evolved into pure entertainment music, now performed by professional musicians in contexts like recording studios, TV, commercial settings, dance halls, and films [67, 18(b)].
  • Specific Theory: These traditions rely on complex non-Western musical systems:
  • India: Music is built upon the concept of rƒÅga (the melodic framework) and tƒÅla (the rhythmic cycle).
  • Arab World: Music uses the maqƒÅm system (melodic modes) and ƒ´qƒÅ'ƒÅt (rhythmic modes).
  • China: The music frequently uses the pentatonic scale.

3.4. Economics & Patronage: The financial systems supporting the music's creation.

The economic support systems vary between traditional patronage and modern commercialism:

  • Patronage (Traditional): In the Indian tradition, many musicians relied on patronage from the wealthy classes to secure employment and perform for auspicious occasions.
  • Commercialisation (Modern): Following the widespread trend in modern music (AOS 4‚Äì7), many groups now perform music commercially. Some Indian groups, for example, perform for tourists at hotels or have become modern concert performers. Chinese Sizhu transitioned to commercial performance in recording studios [67, 18(b)].

3.7. Performance Practice: Traditional ways of interpreting and presenting music.

Understanding how the music is traditionally performed is essential, including instrumentation and style.

  • Mandatory Instrument Recognition: You must learn the sound and function of instruments specific to each ensemble, such as the dizi (flute), erhu (bowed fiddle), and yangqin (hammered dulcimer) for Chinese music, or the sitar, tablƒÅ, and tamb≈´rƒÅ for Hindustani music.
  • Stylistic Techniques: Performance often requires specific techniques to achieve the characteristic sound:
  • Ornamentation: You should recognize the prominent use of vibrato, pitch-bending (portamento), trills, tremolos, and glissandos in all three traditions.
  • Texture: The performance of Chinese and Arab music is predominantly heterophonic (where multiple instruments play slightly varied versions of the same melody simultaneously).
  • Rhythmic Language: The use of unique rhythmic modes is essential, such as ƒ´qƒÅ'ƒÅt (Arab rhythmic mode) and rhythmic patterns associated with the tƒÅla (Indian rhythmic cycle).
